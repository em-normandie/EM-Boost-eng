Introduction to International Trade
Course 1 / 8

1. Definition of International Trade

International trade refers to the exchange of capital, goods and services across national borders. In every international transaction, one country is the exporter and the other the importer. According to Ricardo's principle of comparative advantage, each country benefits from specializing in what it produces best and trading for the rest.

Almost all types of products can be found on the international market: food, clothing, spare parts, oil, jewellery, wine, currencies, water. Services are also traded: tourism, banking, consulting, transport. The tertiary sector (services) has more than quadrupled between 1989 and 2019 with an annual growth rate close to 5%.

💡 The Role of Trade in Development

International trade is a growth engine through: specialization and comparative advantage, poverty reduction through job creation, technology transfer, access to higher-quality goods, and regional and global integration.

2. Growth of World Trade

International trade is 29.6 times larger in 2019 than in 1953, equivalent to an average annual growth rate of 4.96%. Crises have temporarily reduced trade volumes (oil shocks, 2008 crisis, COVID-19), but these reductions have always been temporary.

1. The Market Entry Grid — Market Selection

The market entry grid is the most widely used market selection method. The principle: assign a weight (criterion importance) and a score (market performance), then multiply score × weight. The market with the highest total score is recommended.

Evaluation Criteria:

  • Tax rate
  • Market size (current and future over 3–10 years)
  • Potential market share
  • Purchasing power
  • Complexity of entry
  • Risk of political unrest
  • Trade laws (trade barriers)

5. Other Issues

Parallel Imports (Grey Market)

Entrepreneurs buy goods in the lowest-price country and resell them in the highest-price country through alternative channels. Difficult to combat as it is tied to market characteristics.

Free Trade Zones (FTZ)

Areas of a country with special customs status — considered to be located "outside" the country. Goods can be shipped there without paying duties and without being subject to quotas. Only when goods leave the FTZ to enter the country are they subject to duties.

1. Challenges of International Communication

English is the language of international business, but it is the mother tongue of only ~5% of the world's population. Most business partners communicate in English as a foreign language.

International English (Ed Weiss) — 5 Rules

  • Use unambiguous vocabulary (one word = one meaning)
  • Pay attention to spelling and grammar
  • Write dates unambiguously (e.g. 15 January 2025, not 1/15/25)
  • Use short and clear sentences
  • Avoid sports and military idioms

Special English (Voice of America) — 4 Rules

  • One idea per sentence
  • Vocabulary limited to ~1,500 basic words
  • Speak slowly
  • Rephrase without raising your voice if the other person does not understand

What are Incoterms?

Incoterms® are international trade rules published by the ICC (International Chamber of Commerce). First version: 1936. Current version: Incoterms® 2020 (in force since 1 January 2020). They define 5 elements in a commercial transaction.

1. The 5 Elements Defined by Incoterms

  • Tasks borne by the exporter (transport, export clearance, insurance)
  • Tasks borne by the importer (transport, import clearance, insurance)
  • Costs borne by the exporter
  • Costs borne by the importer
  • Risk transfer point (the moment when risk passes from exporter to importer)

3. Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Common Pitfalls
  • "FOB factory": use FCA instead (FOB = sea only)
  • FOB for air transport: use FCA instead
  • Outdated Incoterms: always specify the version (e.g. CIF Incoterms® 2020)
  • Confusing risk transfer and title transfer: Incoterms do NOT govern the transfer of ownership
  • EXW misunderstood: the exporter has minimum responsibility, but this can create problems for export customs formalities

3. The Letter of Credit (LC) — 10-Step Process

LC Parties
  • Issuing Bank: the importer's bank, issues the LC
  • Advising Bank: the exporter's bank, notifies and may confirm the LC

Phase 1 — Issuance (Steps 1–4)

  1. The importer and exporter agree on the terms of sale (pro forma)
  2. The importer asks its bank to issue a LC
  3. The issuing bank sends the LC to the advising bank
  4. The advising bank informs the exporter of the LC

Phase 2 — Shipment (Steps 5–7)

  1. The exporter ships the goods
  2. The exporter presents documents to its bank
  3. The advising bank checks the documents and sends them to the issuing bank

Phase 3 — Payment (Steps 8–10)

  1. The issuing bank verifies the documents
  2. The issuing bank pays the advising bank
  3. The advising bank pays the exporter

⚠️ Approximately 50% of letters of credit require amendments (documents non-compliant at first presentation).

4. Letter of Credit Variants

  • Standby LC: functions as a guarantee — activated only if the seller is not paid
  • Transferable LC: can be transferred to a second beneficiary (subcontractor)
  • Back-to-back LC: two independent LCs linked to each other
  • Red Clause LC: allows advances to the exporter before shipment
  • Confirmed LC: the advising bank adds its own payment guarantee

5. Documentary Collection

TypeDescription
D/P (Documents against Payment)The importer receives documents (and thus the goods) only after immediate payment. Also called "sight draft".
D/A (Documents against Acceptance)The importer receives documents after accepting a time draft. Deferred payment. Also called "time draft".

📌 Golden Rule

~50% of LCs require an amendment due to documentary discrepancies. The goal: find the method offering the best protection while minimising the risk of losing the sale.

💡 Open Account + Credit Insurance: Best Balance

In practice, Open Account combined with credit insurance often provides the best balance for mature markets: protection against non-payment (risk transferred to insurer) + no risk of losing the sale (conditions similar to domestic trade). Widely used by EU exporters.

5. Transport Modes

Air Transport

  • Accounts for 35% of world value but only 1% of volume
  • ULD (Unit Load Device): special containers for aircraft
  • Frankfurt Cargo City South: largest cargo hub in Europe
  • Advantages: speed, security, schedule reliability
  • Disadvantages: high cost, limited capacity, dangerous goods restrictions

Rail Transport

  • 1 train = 40–50 lorries
  • CO2 reduction of 75% compared to road transport
  • Rhine-Alpine Corridor: Rotterdam to Genoa
  • China-Europe BRI: 13,000 km, 18–20 days (vs 30–40 days sea, vs 5–7 days air)

Sea Transport

  • TEU (Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit): standard container measurement unit
  • Panamax: maximum size for the Panama Canal (approximately 5,000 TEU)
  • Post-Panamax / Ultra Large Container Ships: up to 24,000 TEU
  • Water draft / Air draft: draught and air clearance

🌍 Global transport generates approximately 25% of world CO2 emissions. The EU target is a 30% modal shift from road to rail/waterways by 2030 (TEN-T policy).

6. Types of Maritime Containers

  • Standard: most common, majority of dry goods
  • Refrigerated (reefer): perishable goods with temperature control
  • Open top: goods exceeding standard height
  • Tank container: liquids and gases
  • Flat rack: out-of-gauge cargo
  • Foldable: optimises empty return trips
🚂 Rail Baltica and BRI

China-Europe BRI: Xi'an → Duisburg, 13,000+ km, 18–20 days (vs 35–40 by sea). Rail Baltica: 870 km, EU funding €5 billion. Rhine-Alpine Corridor: Rotterdam–Genoa, 30+ trains/week.

3. Gleicher's Formula

D × V × F > R
(Dissatisfaction × Vision × First steps) must exceed Resistance

For change to succeed, the combination of three factors must be greater than the resistance: D = Dissatisfaction with the current situation, V = Clear and attractive vision of the future, F = Concrete and accessible first steps.

⚠️ If any of the 3 factors is zero (D=0 or V=0 or F=0), the result is zero — change cannot start.

Fundamental Principle

In accordance with the separation of powers, the French judiciary is independent of the legislature, even though its members are civil servants.

2. The Civil Code (Code Napoléon, 1804)

The foundation of the French legal system is the Civil Code, written in 1804. It defines citizens' rights and obligations, laws on property, contracts, inheritance, etc. It adapts Roman law and customary law principles to the needs of 19th-century France.

Laws are proposed by the Government and adopted by both chambers of Parliament. A law comes into force after:

  2. Signature by the President of the Republic

Foundations of Contract Law
Module 2 / 8

1. Definition of a Contract (Art. 1101 CC)

Art. 1101: "A contract is an agreement of wills between two or more persons intended to create, modify, transfer or extinguish obligations."

2. Freedom of Contract — 3 Consequences

PrincipeDescription
ConsensualismThe free and informed will of the parties is sufficient to conclude a contract. No pre-established form required.
Freedom of contractParties are free to contract or not, to choose their counterpart, to determine the content of the contract.
No formal requirementArt. 1128 CC does not make validity conditional on form. The simple exchange of consents is in principle sufficient.
Exception : Contrats Solennels

Certain contracts require specific formalities ad validitatem (for validity): donations, real estate sales, mortgages, home loan contracts, guarantees, collective agreements.

3. Contract Formation — Offer and Acceptance

A. The Offer (Art. 1114 CC)

To be valid, the offer must meet 2 cumulative requirements: Precise (essential elements: nature, subject, price) + Firm (unconditional intent). Otherwise = mere invitation to negotiate.

SituationRègle applicable
Offer with a deadlineThe offeror must maintain the offer during this period. Non-compliance → damages.
Offer without deadline (to the public)The offer is revocable.
Offer without deadline (to an individual)Must be maintained for a "reasonable" period.
Lapse of offerUpon expiry of the fixed or reasonable deadline (e.g. death of the offeror).

B. Acceptance (Art. 1118 CC)

3 conditions: (1) before the offer lapses, (2) pure and simple (no modification), (3) within the deadline. Any modification = counter-offer → no contract formed.

Théorie de la Réception (Art. 1121 CC)

Between persons at a distance: the contract is concluded as soon as the acceptance reaches the offeror. The contract is deemed formed at the place where acceptance is received.

4. The 3 Conditions of Validity (Art. 1128 CC)

ConditionDétailConséquence si défaut
1° Free and informed consentNot vitiated by mistake, duress or fraud. The injured party can prove that without this defect, they would not have contracted.Relative nullity
2° Capacity to contractAny unprotected adult and any emancipated minor. Legal entities have capacity limited by their articles.Nullity of the contract
3° Lawful and certain contentComplies with public order. Obligations possible and determined or determinable.Absolute nullity if contrary to public order

5. Defects of Consent

DefectDefinitionConditions for nullityWhat is NOT a defect
Mistake (Art. 1110 CC)False representation of reality. Ex: buying cutlery believing it is gold when it is brass.Mistake as to the substance of the thing or the person (intuitu personae contracts)Mistake as to value (price too low/high). Material error if the other party acts in good faith.

4. Negotiation Phase and Preliminary Contracts

Negotiation is not mandatory. Withdrawal is free UNLESS carried out in bad faith: advanced-stage negotiation, abrupt termination, without legitimate reason → possible damages (Art. 1112).

Contract Effects & Non-Performance
Module 3 / 8

1. Binding Force (Art. 1103 CC)

Art. 1103: "Contracts lawfully entered into take the place of the law for those who have made them."

Parties are bound to perform the agreed obligations. They may not unilaterally modify or revoke the contract (Art. 1193). They may include a termination clause or a hardship clause (imprévision).

2. The Hardship Doctrine — imprévision (Art. 1195 CC)

If an unforeseeable change of circumstances at the time of conclusion makes performance excessively burdensome:

  1. The party may request renegotiation (while continuing to perform)
  2. In case of refusal/failure: parties may agree on termination, or jointly petition the court for adaptation
  3. If no agreement within a reasonable period: the judge may revise or terminate
Note

This provision has only supplementary status — parties may exclude it when drafting the contract. To be distinguished from force majeure (Art. 1218) which excludes all liability.

3. Relative Effect (Art. 1199 CC)

A contract only creates obligations between the parties. Exception: in a chain of translative contracts, a member may act against another member even without a direct link.

4. The 5 Remedies for Non-Performance (Art. 1217 CC)

SanctionConditionsQui décide ?
1. Plea of non-performanceSuspension of one's own obligation if the other party does not perform. Non-performance sufficiently serious, not used as a means of pressure. Art. 1220: possible in anticipation.The creditor party (under judicial oversight)
2. Specific performanceCompelling the debtor to perform. Exceptions: material impossibility, manifest disproportion. The creditor may have a third party perform after formal notice.Judge (or creditor with formal notice)
3. Price reductionImperfect performance accepted → proportional reduction. Without recourse to a judge.The creditor party (without a judge)
4. Termination/Rescission3 modes: termination clause (automatic) / unilateral rescission (after formal notice, except urgency) / judicial rescission. If obligations partially performed → termination (no retroactive effect).Clause, unilateral decision or judge
5. Dommages-intérêts (Art. 1231-1 CC)"The debtor is ordered to pay damages either on account of non-performance or on account of delay, unless they justify that performance was prevented by force majeure."Juge

6. Settlement Agreement (Art. 2044 CC)

Alternative dispute resolution method: "contract by which the parties, through mutual concessions, end an existing dispute or prevent a future one." It must be in writing and prevents any further action on the same subject.

La Force Majeure
Module 4 / 8

1. Definition (Art. 1218 CC, 2016 Reform)

Art. 1218 CC: "An event beyond the debtor's control, which could not reasonably have been foreseen at the time of contracting and whose effects could not be avoided by appropriate measures."
Historical Context

Although long recognised by the courts, force majeure was only introduced into French law in 2016, following a substantial reform of contract law. To be distinguished from the hardship doctrine (Art. 1195) which concerns economic difficulties.

2. Les 3 Conditions Cumulatives

ConditionTerme juridiqueDescriptionExemple
1. ExtérioritéExtérioritéThe event is beyond the control of the affected party. It does not result from that party or from persons for whom it is responsible (in particular its employees).Un tremblement de terre qui détruit les installations
2. ImprévisibilitéImprévisibilitéThe event could not reasonably have been foreseen at the time of contracting.Une pandémie dont les effets n'étaient pas connus lors de la signature
3. IrrésistibilitéIrrésistibilité (key criterion)The effects could not be avoided by appropriate measures. Assessed in abstracto: could an average person in the same circumstances have performed? As long as performance is possible (even very costly), there is no force majeure.Des restrictions gouvernementales rendant l'exécution légalement impossible

3. Effets de la Force Majeure

SituationLegal consequence
Impossibilité temporairePerformance of obligations is suspended, unless the delay justifies termination of the contract.
Impossibilité définitiveThe contract is terminated by operation of law (without notice or judicial intervention). The parties are deemed to have performed their obligations (Art. 1351 and 1351-1 CC).

4. Le Cas COVID-19

The pandemic must be examined against the 3 criteria, on a case-by-case basis:

  • COVID-19 itself: probably beyond a party's control and potentially unforeseeable (depending on contract date)
  • Key question: was performance truly impossible (and not merely more difficult or costly)?
  • Related measures (lockdowns, imposed closures) more readily satisfy the irresistibility condition

CDI et CDD
Module 5 / 8

1. The CDI — Permanent Employment Contract

The CDI is the most common form of employment contract in France. By definition, it does not specify an end date. It can be full-time or part-time.

Termination methods: resignation, dismissal, mutual termination, retirement.

2. The CDD — Fixed-Term Employment Contract

Règle fondamentale

A CDD may not have the purpose or effect of permanently filling a position linked to the company's normal and permanent activity. It must mandatorily be established in writing.

7 cases authorising use of a CDD:

  1. Replacement of an absent employee
  2. Replacement of an employee temporarily working part-time
  3. Pending a new employee taking up their position
  4. Pending the definitive elimination of an employee's position
  5. Temporary increase in company activity
  6. Nature of the activity (moving, survey, teaching…)
  7. Seasonal employment

CDD Renewal:

  • Maximum 2 times
  • Total duration (including renewals) ≤ 18 months
  • Cumulative conditions: clause provided in the contract + proposal before the end of the initial contract

End-of-Contract Bonus:

At the end of a CDD (if not followed by a CDI): minimum 10% of total gross remuneration. May be reduced to 6% by an extended sector agreement, in exchange for privileged access to vocational training.

3. CDD Renewal — Strict Rules

🚨 Règles à connaître — CDD
  • Maximum 2 renewals
  • Total duration (initial CDD + renewals) ≤ 18 months
  • Two cumulative conditions: clause provided in the contract + proposal before the end
  • Violation → reclassification as CDI by the judge
  • CDI termination methods: resignation, dismissal, mutual termination, retirement, job abandonment (presumed resignation since 2023)
⚠️ Job Abandonment — Since 2023

Since the law of 21 December 2022 (in force March 2023): an employee who abandons their post and does not return to work after a formal notice from the employer is presumed to have resigned. Consequence: loses the right to unemployment benefits (unlike a dismissal). The employer may set the response deadline in the formal notice (minimum 15 days).

1. Main Obligations of the Employee (5)

Clause de Non-Concurrence
Module 8 / 8

1. Definition and Principles

The non-competition clause aims to limit an employee's freedom to work, after termination of their contract, in equivalent roles for a competitor or on their own account. It is not defined by law, but clarified by case law. It must be drawn up in writing.

Distinction importante

Non-competition clause → applies AFTER termination of the contract.
Inherent non-competition obligation → applies DURING performance of the contract (in all employment contracts).

2. The 4 Cumulative Validity Conditions

⚠️ The absence of just one condition renders the clause null and void
N°ConditionExplication
1Essential to protecting legitimate interestsJustified by the nature of the role (customer contact, access to confidential information). The more ordinary the role, the less justifiable the clause.
2Limited in time and spaceDuration and geographical scope precisely defined and proportionate. A clause covering all of France for 5 years would likely be excessive.
3Taking into account the specificities of the employmentThe employer must consider the constraints imposed on the employee and their ability to find new employment. If the role is highly specialised, the compensation must be high.
4Non-derisory financial compensationIn practice: at least ⅓ of the last monthly salary (Cass. soc., 15 Nov. 2006, n° 04-46.271). Derisory compensation = no compensation = clause is null and void.

3. Terms & Waiver

The financial compensation must be paid AFTER termination of the employment contract (Cass. soc., 17 Nov. 2010, n° 09-42.389), not during its performance.

The employer may waive the clause under the conditions provided in the contract or with the employee's agreement. The waiver must be notified by registered letter with acknowledgement of receipt, clear and not subject to interpretation.

4. Consequences of Non-Compliance

Partie en fauteConséquences
Employee failing to comply with the clausePayment of compensation ceases. Possible order to pay damages.
Employer failing to pay compensationThe employee is no longer bound by the clause. The employer remains liable for the period during which the employee complied.
Clause not meeting the 4 conditionsClause is null, possible revision, or compensation for harm suffered by the employee.

1. Which Test to Use?

SituationVariables impliquéesTest approprié
Compare a mean to a reference value (N ≥ 30)1 variable quantitative, 1 valeur μ₀Test Z (loi normale)
Compare a mean to a reference value (N < 30)1 variable quantitative, 1 valeur μ₀Test Student (loi t, ddl = N–1)
Compare means across several groups (>2)1 variable quantitative + 1 variable qualitativeANOVA (test F)
Test independence between 2 qualitative variables2 variables qualitatives (tableau contingence)Test du Chi-2 (χ²)
Measure linear association between 2 quantitative variables2 variables quantitativesCorrélation (r de Pearson)

2. The 4 Universal Steps of a Statistical Test

Method to ALWAYS apply
  1. Formulate hypotheses: H₀ (null hypothesis, "no difference") and H₁ (alternative hypothesis, "there is a difference")
  2. Choose the test and calculate the observed statistic: Z_obs, t_obs, F_obs, χ²_obs, r depending on context
  3. Determine the critical value: from the table (df, threshold α, two-tailed/one-tailed)
  4. Make the decision: If stat_obs > stat_crit → REJECT H₀ (significant difference) / If stat_obs ≤ stat_crit → DO NOT REJECT H₀

3. The 8 Pitfalls to Avoid in the Exam

PiègeRègle
Large vs small sampleN ≥ 30 → Z (normal) | N < 30 → t (Student, df=N–1)
Two-tailed vs one-tailedTwo-tailed: α split across 2 tails. One-tailed (right): all α in the right tail.
Chi-2: expected frequencieseij = (row total × column total) / N total. Each eij should ideally be ≥ 5.
ANOVA: divide by dfMS_A = SS_A / (k–1) and MS_E = SS_E / (N–k). Don't forget the division!
Regression: reference categoryAlways identify the reference category before interpreting coefficients.
Sign of regression coefficient+ means increase relative to reference, – means decrease.
R² vs rR² = coefficient of determination (overall explanation). r = correlation (linear relationship between 2 vars.). R² = r² for simple regression.
t/Z decisionDo not confuse |t_obs| with t_obs. For one-tailed (right) test: if t_obs is positive, compare directly to t_crit.

5. Key Formula Reminder

Empirical std dev: s = √[Σni(xi–x̄)² / (n–1)]
Z-Test (large sample): Z_obs = (x̄ – μ₀) / (s / √N)
T-Test (small sample): t_obs = (x̄ – μ₀) / (s / √N) with df = N–1
Chi-2: χ² = Σ(nij – eij)² / eij where eij = (row total × col. total) / N
ANOVA: F_obs = MS_A / MS_E where MS_A = SS_A/(k–1) and MS_E = SS_E/(N–k)
Pearson Correlation: r = Σ(xi–x̄)(yi–ȳ) / √[Σ(xi–x̄)² × Σ(yi–ȳ)²]

1. Le Concept de Luxe

Le terme « luxe » vient du latin luxus (évoquant la débauche, l'excès, le faste) — connotation initialement négative. La notion a évolué au fil des siècles pour désigner un idéal d'excellence et de raffinement.

📖 Définition du Luxe

Produits ou services offrant une qualité supérieure, une exclusivité et un prix élevé, associés au prestige et au statut. Répond à des besoins superflus — non une nécessité de base. Fruit de la créativité, presque directement lié à l'art.

"Luxury is the opposite of vulgarity." — Gabrielle Chanel

"Luxury is creating a dream that lasts." — Jean-Louis Dumas (Hermès)

1. The Role of Pricing in Luxury

Dans le luxe, le prix ne couvre pas simplement les coûts — il joue un rôle vital dans la perception de la marque, la désirabilité et l'exclusivité. Un prix élevé agit comme un symbole de statut.
